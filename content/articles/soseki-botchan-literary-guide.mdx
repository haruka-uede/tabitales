Natsume Soseki's *Botchan* (坊っちゃん, 1906) is one of the most widely read novels in Japan — a short, fast, comic story about a hot-tempered young Tokyoite who takes a teaching post at a middle school in the provinces and clashes with nearly everyone he meets. It's closely based on Soseki's own miserable, funny year teaching English in Matsuyama, on the island of Shikoku, in 1895 — and Matsuyama has embraced the connection so thoroughly that "Botchan" now names a train, a dessert, a clock, and half the city's souvenir shelf.

This guide follows the real sites behind the novel's thinly fictionalized Matsuyama, with practical notes for visiting.

## Who was Natsume Soseki?

Soseki (1867–1916) is generally considered the most important novelist of modern Japan — his face appeared on the ¥1,000 note for nearly two decades. Before he was a full-time writer, he taught English literature, including the single unhappy year in Matsuyama that became the raw material for *Botchan*. Almost every novel of his maps onto a real place he lived or visited, which makes his bibliography an unusually good excuse for a trip — this guide is the first in a series following his major work around Japan.

## Stop 1: Dogo Onsen Honkan (道後温泉本館) — the bathhouse Soseki barely bothered to disguise

*Botchan* never actually says "Dogo Onsen" — the narrator calls it "Sumita no Onsen" (住田の温泉), a fig leaf so thin that every reader in Matsuyama immediately knew what he meant. Dogo Onsen Honkan, the wooden bathhouse at the center of the story, is Japan's oldest hot spring bathhouse still in operation, first built in 1894 and now an Important Cultural Property. Inside the Kami-no-yu bath, a wooden sign still reads **"Botchan, no swimming"** (坊っちゃん泳ぐべからず) — a nod to the scene where the narrator gets in trouble for doing laps in the tub. A small "Botchan Room" upstairs displays photos and a bust of Soseki, along with material on his friend, the poet Masaoka Shiki, who was also from Matsuyama.

- **Getting there**: Dogo Onsen Station, the terminus of the Iyotetsu tram, then a 4-minute walk through a covered shopping arcade. From JR Matsuyama Station, take tram Line 5 (about 20 minutes); from Matsuyama-shi Station, Line 3 (about 15 minutes).
- **Good to know**: The Honkan underwent a multi-year seismic renovation and reopened fully in December 2024, so bathing halls and rest rooms should be completely open — but it's worth a quick check before you go, in case anything has shifted again.

## Stop 2: The Botchan Train and Karakuri Clock, Dogo Onsen Station

Right outside Dogo Onsen Station, two smaller attractions round out the theme. The **Botchan Train** (坊っちゃん列車) is a diesel-powered replica of the 1888 steam locomotives that ran Matsuyama's original tram line — retired in 1931, reintroduced as a tourist tram in 2001, complete with a whistle and simulated smoke. It runs on two loop routes connecting Dogo Onsen with Okaido and both of Matsuyama's main train stations. Just across the plaza, the **Botchan Karakuri Clock** (坊っちゃんカラクリ時計), built in 1994, rises and animates with characters from the novel on the hour.

- **Getting there**: Both are right at Dogo Onsen Station — the clock is in Hojoen park directly in front of it.
- **Good to know**: The Botchan Train currently runs weekends and holidays only, and costs considerably more than a normal tram (around ¥1,300 versus the ¥180 flat tram fare) — check the current schedule before planning a ride around it. The clock performs on the hour from roughly 8am to 10pm, with extra half-hour shows on weekends and in peak seasons.

## Stop 3: Matsuyama Castle

*Botchan* doesn't send its narrator up to the castle directly, but it looms over every description of the city he's stuck teaching in, and it's one of only twelve Japanese castles with an original, non-reconstructed keep — worth the detour on its own merits.

- **Getting there**: Ropeway or chairlift from Choja-ya Station, a 5-minute walk from the Okaido tram stop; the ropeway takes about 10 minutes, the chairlift about 6, for the same fare.
- **Good to know**: The chairlift is open-air and single-seat — take the ropeway instead if it's raining.

## Stop 4: The former Matsuyama Middle School site

The school where Soseki actually taught in 1895 is long gone — the site is now occupied by an NTT West office — but a small marker in Ichibancho notes the connection for anyone tracing his real footsteps rather than the novel's fictionalized version of the school.

- **Getting there**: A 2-minute walk from the Shiyakusho-mae ("City Hall") tram stop.
- **Good to know**: There's little to see beyond the marker itself — treat this as a five-minute add-on for completists rather than a destination in its own right.

## Practical travel notes

- **Best time to visit**: Matsuyama is pleasant most of the year; spring cherry blossoms at the castle and autumn foliage are the two peak windows if you want the city at its most photogenic.
- **Getting to Matsuyama**: From Tokyo, fly into Matsuyama Airport (about 90 minutes from Haneda) or take the Shinkansen to Okayama and transfer to the JR Shiokaze limited express (around 6.5 hours total). From Osaka, Shinkansen to Okayama plus the Shiokaze runs about 4 hours, or fly direct. Airport buses connect Matsuyama Airport to JR Matsuyama Station (about 15 minutes) and on to Dogo Onsen (about 35 minutes).
- **Base yourself in**: Dogo Onsen itself — nearly everything in this guide is walkable or a short tram ride from there.
