*Kokoro* (こころ, "The Heart," 1914) is Soseki's best-known novel in Japan and often the first one assigned in Japanese high schools: a young narrator befriends an older man he calls only "Sensei," and gradually uncovers the guilt Sensei has carried since a betrayal in his own youth. The novel opens on a beach in Kamakura and closes around a Tokyo grave — and unusually for this series, one of those two real places is also where Soseki himself ended up.

## Stop 1: Yuigahama Beach, Kamakura — where the narrator meets Sensei

The novel opens with the narrator, on summer vacation, swimming at Yuigahama in Kamakura, where he first notices Sensei standing apart from the crowd. This is the mainstream, most-cited real-world identification for the beach — Japanese scholarship generally names Yuigahama directly, though a smaller number of local sources argue for neighboring Zaimokuza Beach instead, since the text itself never names the beach outright. Either way, this is the stretch of coast to visit; the two beaches sit right next to each other.

- **Getting there**: Enoshima Electric Railway ("Enoden") to Yuigahama Station, about 4–5 minutes' walk to the sand. From Kamakura Station, it's one stop on the Enoden (about 5 minutes) or a direct 20–25 minute walk.
- **Good to know**: Kamakura is a popular day trip from Tokyo on its own merits, so pair this stop with the temples and shrines the city is already known for rather than treating it as a single-purpose literary errand.

## Stop 2: Zoshigaya Cemetery, Tokyo — where the novel and Soseki's real life converge

Later in the novel, Sensei visits the grave of his late friend K every month at a cemetery in Zoshigaya, Tokyo — the text names the location directly. What makes this stop unusual is that Natsume Soseki is himself actually buried here: an unusually tall, armchair-shaped tomb marks his grave in Section 1, Block 14. Some literary analysis has even pointed out that the novel's description of visiting the cemetery echoes near-verbatim passages from Soseki's own diary, written after visiting his young daughter's grave at the same cemetery years before he wrote the book — a real grief folded almost directly into the fiction.

- **Getting there**: The Toden-Zoshigaya stop on the Tokyo Sakura Tram (Arakawa Line) is closest, about 2 minutes' walk. Zoshigaya Station (Fukutoshin Line, Exit 1) is about 8 minutes; Higashi-Ikebukuro Station (Yurakucho Line) about 7 minutes.
- **Good to know**: The cemetery is large and quiet, with several other notable graves if you wander, including Lafcadio Hearn and novelist Kyoka Izumi. A cemetery map near the entrance makes finding Soseki's grave straightforward.

## Stop 3 (optional): Koishikawa, Tokyo — Sensei's inferred neighborhood

The novel never names Sensei's neighborhood outright, but in-text walking routes — past Denzu-in Temple, around the edge of a botanical garden, toward Tomisaka — have led readers to place his house in Koishikawa, in present-day Bunkyo Ward. That botanical garden is real and open to the public: the **Koishikawa Botanical Garden**, run by the University of Tokyo, is a genuinely pleasant place to walk regardless of the literary connection.

- **Getting there**: About a 10-minute walk from Myogadani Station (Marunouchi Line) or Hakusan Station (Toei Mita Line).
- **Good to know**: This stop is inference, not a stated location in the text — treat it as a bonus for readers who want to keep walking Sensei's Tokyo rather than a must-see.

## Practical travel notes

- **Base yourself in**: Tokyo, with Kamakura as a day trip — the JR Yokosuka Line runs direct, no transfer, in about 55–60 minutes.
- **Getting around**: Standard IC cards (Suica/Pasmo) work at every station in this guide.
- **Best time to visit**: Kamakura gets crowded on summer weekends, when the beach itself is in full swing; a weekday visit, or an off-season one focused on the temples and Zoshigaya, is quieter.
