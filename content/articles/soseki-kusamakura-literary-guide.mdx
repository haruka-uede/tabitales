*Kusamakura* (草枕, "The Grass Pillow," 1906) is Soseki at his least plot-driven and most atmospheric: a nameless painter walks into the mountains looking for a place to think about beauty, and mostly just does that, for a short novel's worth of pages. It's based on a real trip Soseki took at the end of 1897, while he was teaching in Kumamoto, to a hot-spring hamlet then called Oama — today part of Tamana City, a quiet, little-visited corner of Kumamoto Prefecture that still calls itself "Kusamakura no Sato," the village of Kusamakura.

This is the least convenient stop in this series to actually reach, and also the one where the real geography lines up most precisely with the fiction — worth the detour if you want to see a Soseki setting that's barely changed.

## Stop 1: Maeda Villa, Oama Onsen — the house behind the novel

The painter in *Kusamakura* stays at a real house: the retirement villa of Maeda Kakashi, a Meiji-era Freedom and People's Rights politician, where Soseki himself was a guest at the end of 1897. The villa survives — now preserved within the **Kusamakura Exchange Hall** (草枕交流館) in Oama — with its six-mat guest room and an old bathhouse, restored in 2005, still intact. The garden pond beside the house is the acknowledged real-world model for **Kagami-ga-ike** ("Mirror Pond"), one of the novel's recurring images.

- **Getting there**: There's no train to Oama. From Kumamoto City, the most reliable route is by car (roughly 40–50 minutes); a Sanko Bus service runs toward "Oama Onsen," but departures are sparse — as few as one round trip a day on some schedules — so check current timetables carefully before relying on it. Alternatively, take the JR Kagoshima Line to Tamana Station (about 20–30 minutes from Kumamoto) and continue by taxi (roughly 11km, 20–25 minutes).
- **Good to know**: The villa has been through recent renovation closures, so call ahead (0968-82-4511) or check current hours before making the trip out — this is remote enough that showing up to a closed building is a real risk, not just an inconvenience.

## Stop 2: Kusamakura Onsen Tensui

A short walk from the villa, this public bathhouse-and-exhibition facility leans fully into the novel's namesake, with a bath called "Kusamakura no Yu" and an observation tower looking out over the Ariake Sea toward Mt. Unzen. It's a good, low-key way to actually soak in the setting rather than just look at it.

- **Getting there**: Walking distance from the Maeda Villa / Kusamakura Exchange Hall in Oama.
- **Good to know**: Admission is inexpensive, around ¥500. Bring a translation app — there's minimal English signage this far outside Kumamoto City.

## Stop 3: The old Kumamoto–Oama road

The novel's mountain-pass teahouse scene draws on real teahouses that once lined the old road between Kumamoto and Oama, over the Toriigoe and Noide passes. Little remains to formally visit, but if you're traveling by car rather than taxi, following this older route rather than the main road gives a better sense of the walk the painter — and Soseki — actually made.

- **Getting there**: Only practical by car; not signposted as a tourist route.
- **Good to know**: This is a nice-to-have for readers who want to trace the full journey, not a required stop.

## Practical travel notes

- **Best time to visit**: Spring, when Tensui's plum and peach blossoms are in bloom (the area is locally nicknamed for its "peach blossom spring" feel), or autumn. Avoid Kyushu's rainy season in June.
- **Base yourself in**: Kumamoto City — Oama itself has almost no lodging beyond the small ryokan Nakoi-kan, a Meiji-era inn that took its name from the novel's fictional setting, Nagoi Onsen.
- **Language**: This is rural Kyushu with very little English signage — a translation app is essential here, more so than for any other stop in this series.
