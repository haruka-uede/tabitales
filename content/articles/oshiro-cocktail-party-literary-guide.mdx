*Cocktail Party* (カクテル・パーティー) is Tatsuhiro Oshiro's 1967 novella, the first work by an Okinawan-born author to win the Akutagawa Prize. Set during the long U.S. military administration of Okinawa that followed the Battle of Okinawa and lasted until 1972, it follows a Japanese man whose friendly ties with an American acquaintance unravel after his daughter is assaulted by a U.S. serviceman. The case he brings against the men involved exposes exactly how little protection Okinawan civilians actually had under the legal arrangements of the occupation, no matter how warmly "friendship" was performed at parties like the one that opens the book.

## A novel of occupation-era Okinawa, from the inside

Oshiro was born in Nakagusuku Village, central Okinawa, in 1925, and lived through the Battle of Okinawa and the 27 years of U.S. administration that followed before reversion to Japan in 1972 — the same world *Cocktail Party* is set in. The novella opens at a friendship party inside a U.S. military housing compound, where a Japanese, an Okinawan, a Chinese, and an American character perform the easy goodwill of the occasion; that surface is what collapses over the course of the book, as the protagonist discovers how quickly those same friendships evaporate once an Okinawan actually needs them to hold.

## Stop 1: Michi-no-Eki Kadena — seeing the scale the novel takes for granted

No single building is confirmed as a location from the novel, but understanding *Cocktail Party* means understanding just how much of Okinawa the U.S. military occupies — a reality the book assumes its readers already live with, and one many visitors don't grasp until they see it. Michi-no-Eki Kadena, a roadside station in Kadena Town, is the only public facility in Japan with a direct view into an active U.S. base: its 4th-floor observation deck looks straight out over Kadena Air Base, the largest U.S. base in Japan, while a 3rd-floor exhibition room (opened 2022) documents Kadena Town's history before, during, and after the war — including the fact that roughly 82% of the town's land is still occupied by the base today.

- **Getting there**: About 30 minutes by car or taxi from Naha; no direct train service reaches Kadena, so a rental car or taxi is the practical option.
- **Good to know**: Fighter jet takeoffs and landings are visible, and audible, from the deck at unpredictable times. The exhibition room is a calmer, more informative stop if the noise is more than you'd bargained for.

## Stop 2: Sakima Art Museum, Ginowan — land taken by a base, and taken back

The Sakima Art Museum sits on a small parcel of land inside the perimeter of Futenma Air Station — land that belonged to the family of museum director Michio Sakima before the war, requisitioned into the base afterward, and returned only after Sakima negotiated its release. He opened the museum there in 1994. Its centerpiece is *The Battle of Okinawa* (沖縄戦の図), a large-scale permanent mural by Iri and Toshi Maruki, the same husband-and-wife artists known for their *Hiroshima Panels*; the building's rooftop staircase was deliberately aligned to the sunset on June 23, Okinawa's Irei no Hi memorial day marking the end of the battle, and offers its own view over Futenma's runways.

- **Getting there**: About 30–50 minutes by car from Naha; the nearest bus stop, Uehara, is about a 5-minute walk from the museum.
- **Good to know**: The museum is small and easy to combine with a Kadena visit on the same day, since both sit in central Okinawa.

## Practical travel notes

- **Best time to visit**: Any season works for both stops, though June 23 (Irei no Hi) gives the Sakima Art Museum's rooftop a significance it doesn't have on an ordinary day.
- **Base yourself in**: Naha, Okinawa's capital, is the practical hub for reaching both Kadena and Ginowan.
- **Language**: Michi-no-Eki Kadena's exhibition room has some English signage; expect mostly Japanese elsewhere at both stops.
- **The book itself**: the standard English translation, titled *Cocktail Party*, is by Steve Rabson, collected in *Okinawa: Two Postwar Novellas*.
