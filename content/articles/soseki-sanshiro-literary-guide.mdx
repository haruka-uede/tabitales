*Sanshiro* (三四郎, 1908) follows a young man who leaves rural Kyushu for Tokyo to attend Tokyo Imperial University — today's University of Tokyo — and spends the novel being quietly overwhelmed by the city, the university, and a young woman named Mineko. Unusually for this series, you don't need to reconstruct where the novel is set: the campus is unchanged enough, and the connection embraced officially enough, that one of its ponds is literally named after the book.

This is the shortest, easiest trip in this series — a single central Tokyo campus, freely open to the public, following almost exactly the route Sanshiro himself takes on arrival.

## Stop 1: Ochanomizu to Akamon — Sanshiro's own route in

In the novel, Sanshiro gets off the tram at Ochanomizu, hails a rickshaw, and has it "pulled briskly through the Red Gate" on his way into the university grounds — a real, specific line, not a reader's inference. The **Akamon** ("Red Gate," 赤門), built in 1827 and now an Important Cultural Property, is still standing and is Hongo campus's best-known landmark.

- **Getting there**: Ochanomizu Station (JR Chuo/Sobu Lines or Marunouchi/Chiyoda Metro lines) if you want to trace Sanshiro's exact starting point; otherwise Hongo-sanchome Station (Marunouchi or Toei Oedo Line) is a direct 6–8 minute walk to the gate itself.
- **Good to know**: As of this writing, Akamon is closed to passage for a multi-year restoration (November 2025–September 2027) — you can see and photograph it from outside, but can't walk through it the way Sanshiro does. Enter campus through one of the other open gates nearby instead.

## Stop 2: Sanshiro Pond (三四郎池)

Officially Ikutoku-en Shinji-ike (育徳園心字池), a garden pond dating to 1638 when the site belonged to the Maeda clan, this became "Sanshiro Pond" in popular usage because it's where Sanshiro first meets Mineko in the novel. The text doesn't pin the meeting to one exact, identifiable spot matching the pond as it exists today, so treat this as the traditionally accepted setting rather than a precise scene location — but it's a genuinely lovely, quiet corner of the campus regardless.

- **Getting there**: A short walk from Akamon into the campus interior; alternatively, Todaimae Station (Namboku Line) puts you about 1 minute from the main gate, with the pond another 5–10 minutes' walk in.
- **Good to know**: The pond and surrounding campus are free and open to the public for self-guided walking, generally 7am–6pm daily. Campus access is restricted on Japan's Common Test day and the university's own entrance exam days in January and late February — dates shift year to year, so check before visiting in that window.

## Practical travel notes

- **Best time to visit**: Mid-November to early December for autumn foliage around the pond, or spring for fresh green leaves — both are more atmospheric than midsummer.
- **Base yourself in**: Anywhere in central Tokyo — Hongo is a short trip from most neighborhoods, and this pairs easily with a single afternoon.
- **Language**: Central Tokyo, well within normal tourist infrastructure.
