Osamu Dazai's 1944 travelogue-memoir *Tsugaru* is a quiet, wandering account of his return to his home region in northern Aomori after years away in Tokyo. Unlike his darker, more famous novel *No Longer Human*, *Tsugaru* is warm, funny, and deeply tied to real, visitable places — which makes it one of the best starting points for anyone who wants to combine a love of Japanese literature with a trip off the well-trodden Tokyo–Kyoto–Osaka route.

This guide follows Dazai's own route through Tsugaru, with practical notes for planning a visit.

## Who was Osamu Dazai?

Dazai (1909–1948) was born into a wealthy landowning family in Kanagi, a small town in rural Aomori Prefecture, at the northern tip of Japan's main island. He's best known internationally for *No Longer Human*, but *Tsugaru* is arguably a better introduction to the region he came from — and, as a public domain work, it's also freely available in translation and widely discussed without the copyright restrictions that apply to more recent authors.

## Stop 1: Dazai Osamu Kinenkan "Shayokan" (斜陽館), Kanagi

The single most important stop for any Dazai pilgrimage is his childhood home in Kanagi, now preserved as the **Dazai Osamu Memorial Hall, known as Shayokan** ("Villa of the Setting Sun" — also the title of another of his novels, *The Setting Sun*). The building itself is a striking early-20th-century mansion, reflecting just how wealthy his family was, and now functions as a museum with manuscripts, personal items, and period rooms open to the public.

- **Getting there**: Kanagi is reached via the Tsugaru Railway from Goshogawara Station. The Tsugaru Railway's "Stove Train" (running in winter) is itself a well-known local attraction.
- **Good to know**: Give yourself at least half a day — Kanagi is small, and the memorial hall is best paired with a slow walk around the town Dazai describes in the book.

## Stop 2: Goshogawara

Goshogawara is the transit hub for the region and appears in *Tsugaru* as Dazai passes through on his journey. It's a practical base for travelers, with better rail and bus connections than Kanagi itself.

## Stop 3: Kodomari, the northern coast

In the book's most emotionally resonant section, Dazai travels to the remote coastal village of **Kodomari** to reconnect with Take, the woman who cared for him as a child. The windswept, end-of-the-road feeling of this coastline is very much still there today, and it's a striking contrast to the manicured, mansion-like feel of Shayokan.

## Practical travel notes

- **Best time to visit**: Late April–May (cherry blossoms) or the Tsugaru Railway's winter Stove Train season (December–March) both give the region a strong sense of atmosphere.
- **Base yourself in**: Hirosaki or Aomori City, both well connected by train, with Tsugaru's Dazai sites reachable as a day trip.
- **Language**: This is rural Japan — English signage is limited outside major stations, so a translation app is useful.
