*Kafka on the Shore* (海辺のカフカ, 2002) follows fifteen-year-old Kafka Tamura as he runs away from his father's home in Nakano, Tokyo, and travels to Takamatsu, on the island of Shikoku, where he takes refuge in a private library. It's one of Murakami's most internationally read novels — and one where the author has been unusually direct about what's real and what isn't.

## The library that (officially) doesn't exist

The Komura Memorial Library, where Kafka spends much of the novel, is central enough to the book that readers have spent two decades trying to find it in real Takamatsu. Murakami has settled the question himself, writing in a companion book of reader correspondence that the library is entirely invented: "the Komura Memorial Library certainly exists — that's a lie. It doesn't exist. I made it up myself."

That hasn't stopped the search. The most-cited candidate isn't in Takamatsu at all, but in the neighboring city of Sakaide: the **Kamada Kyosaikai Kyodo Museum** (鎌田共済会郷土博物館), a 1922 building that operated as a private library founded by a soy sauce manufacturer's charitable foundation, with a display room for paintings and calligraphy on its upper floor — a structural echo of the novel's fictional library, founded by a wealthy family and housing an art collection.

## Stop 1: Kamada Kyosaikai Kyodo Museum, Sakaide

- **Getting there**: About 5 minutes on foot from JR Sakaide Station (Yosan Line) — roughly 20–30 minutes by train from Takamatsu.
- **Good to know**: Free admission, open 9:30–16:30 (last entry 16:00), closed Mondays, public holidays, August 13–15, and December 29–January 4. It's a genuine local history museum in its own right, not a shrine to the novel — go for the building and the collection, not expecting any explicit Murakami connection on site.

## In the novel: Takamatsu and Nakano

Takamatsu itself — Kafka's destination, and Shikoku's largest city — is real and worth wandering regardless of the library question; the novel doesn't tie him to other specific, identifiable addresses beyond it. Nakano, Tokyo, where Kafka starts, is a real, dense central-Tokyo ward with no particular site tied to the book.

## Practical travel notes

- **Best time to visit**: Takamatsu and Sakaide are pleasant most of the year; spring and autumn avoid Shikoku's hottest, most humid stretch.
- **Base yourself in**: Takamatsu, with Sakaide as an easy half-day side trip by train.
- **The book itself**: the standard English translation is Philip Gabriel's.
