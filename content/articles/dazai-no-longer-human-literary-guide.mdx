*No Longer Human* (人間失格, *Ningen Shikkaku*) is Osamu Dazai's most famous novel, and one of the best-selling works in modern Japanese literature. Where *Tsugaru* shows Dazai at his warmest, revisiting the rural hometown he loved, *No Longer Human* is almost entirely urban and unsparing — a confession narrated through a young man's descent into alcoholism, addiction, and isolation. It was serialized in the magazine *Tenbo* in the summer of 1948; Dazai completed the manuscript that May and died by suicide in June, before the final installment had even reached readers. Read alongside [our guide to *Tsugaru*](/articles/dazai-tsugaru-literary-guide), it shows the other side of the same writer.

This guide follows the two real places most directly tied to the novel and to Dazai's own life: the Kamakura coast behind its darkest scene, and the Tokyo neighborhood where he wrote it and is buried.

## The story behind the notebooks

The novel is framed as three notebooks written by a man named Yōzō, bracketed by a short preface and afterword narrated by someone else entirely. In the afterword, the narrator tracks down the notebooks at a bar in **Kyobashi**, central Tokyo, where the bar's madam had kept them for over a decade. Yōzō's friend Horiki is introduced as being from **Asakusa**. Neither neighborhood has a dedicated Dazai site today, but both are easy, atmospheric detours if you're already exploring central Tokyo.

## Stop 1: Koyurugi-misaki, Kamakura — the cape behind the novel's turning point

The novel's pivotal scene has Yōzō and a bar hostess named Tsuneko attempt to drown themselves together in the sea at Kamakura; only Yōzō survives. Dazai didn't invent this from nothing — in November 1930, as a 21-year-old university student, he and a 19-year-old Ginza bar hostess named Shimeko Tanabe took an overdose of sleeping pills at **Koyurugi-misaki**, a small cape in Kamakura's Koshigoe area. Tanabe died; Dazai survived and was never prosecuted.

Koyurugi-misaki is still there, and still worth the trip regardless of the literary connection: a small point between Shichirigahama and Koshigoe beaches, with Koyurugi Shrine at its tip and an observation deck looking out toward Enoshima.

- **Getting there**: Koshigoe Station on the Enoden line, about 5 minutes on foot.
- **Good to know**: The novel itself doesn't name the exact spot — it just says "the sea at Kamakura" — so treat this as the real-life site behind the scene rather than a location described in the text. Come near sunset if you can; it's a popular spot for exactly that reason.

## Stop 2: Mitaka, Tokyo — where the book was written, and where Dazai's story ended

Dazai lived in Mitaka, a residential area in western Tokyo, from 1939 until his death in 1948 — the most productive stretch of his career, and the period that produced both *No Longer Human* and *Tsugaru*. Weeks after finishing the manuscript, he drowned himself in the Tamagawa Josui canal that runs through the neighborhood. Several sites within a short walk of Mitaka Station cover this whole arc:

- **Dazai Osamu Literary Salon (太宰治文学サロン)** — 3-16-14 Shimorenjaku, Mitaka; about 3 minutes on foot from the station's south exit. Open 10:00–17:30, closed one day a week. Manuscript reproductions, early editions, and photos from his Mitaka years; a guided literary walking tour runs on the fourth Sunday of each month.
- **Tamagawa Josui / "Kaze no Sanpo-michi" (風の散歩道)** — the canal where Dazai and Tomie Yamazaki drowned themselves, now a shaded walking path from the station's south exit toward Kichijoji. A stone marker, Tamashika-ishi (玉鹿石), sits near where their bodies were recovered.
- **Zenrin-ji Temple (禅林寺)** — 4-18-20 Shimorenjaku, Mitaka; free, open 9:00–17:00. Dazai's grave is here, facing that of novelist Mori Ogai, whom he admired. The temple holds an annual memorial, Ōka-ki (桜桃忌), every June 19 — the day his body was found.
- **Mitaka train yard footbridge (三鷹電車庫跨線橋)** — about 5 minutes from the station's west side. A spot Dazai was fond of and mentioned in his other writing; on clear days you can see Mt. Fuji past the rail yard.

## Practical travel notes

- **Best time to visit**: Kamakura gets crowded on summer weekends — a weekday or off-season visit makes Koyurugi-misaki much quieter. If the Ōka-ki memorial on June 19 interests you, expect a busier-than-usual Zenrin-ji.
- **Base yourself in**: central Tokyo works for both. Mitaka is about 20 minutes from Shinjuku by train; Kamakura is roughly an hour from central Tokyo, doable as a day trip.
- **Language**: signage is in English at all the stations and sites above — this is a much easier route logistically than the rural Tsugaru trip.
- **The book itself**: the standard English translation is Donald Keene's, published by New Directions.
