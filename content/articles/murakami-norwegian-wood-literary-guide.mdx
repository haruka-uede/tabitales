*Norwegian Wood* (ノルウェイの森, 1987) is Murakami's breakthrough novel and, for many international readers, their first — a straightforward, realist coming-of-age story about Toru Watanabe, caught between two very different women while a student in late-1960s Tokyo. Unlike Murakami's more surreal work, its geography is grounded and mostly real, even where the text itself stays coy about exact names.

## Stop 1: Waseda University, Tokyo

The novel never names Watanabe's university, but the campus described — and Murakami's own biography — point unmistakably to Waseda, where Murakami himself studied. When the book was adapted into a 2010 film, Waseda leaned into the connection rather than away from it, hosting the Japan premiere at its Okuma Auditorium and opening real campus locations to the production, including Okuma Garden (大隈庭園) and the Tsubouchi Memorial Theatre Museum, which appears in the background of the film's student-protest scenes.

- **Getting there**: Waseda Station (Tokyo Metro Tozai Line) or Takadanobaba Station (JR Yamanote Line, Seibu Shinjuku Line), both a short walk to the main campus.
- **Good to know**: The campus is open to visitors during the day; Okuma Garden, just behind the auditorium, is the easiest single spot to see and free to enter. Watanabe's dormitory itself is fictional, but the novel's description points toward the Mejirodai/Sekiguchi area near Chinzan-so, historically known for its fireflies — which matters, since the book has a quietly famous firefly scene.

## Stop 2: North of Kyoto, toward the mountains

Naoko is treated at a remote sanatorium called Ami Hostel (阿美寮), which Watanabe reaches by bus from Kyoto's Sanjo, heading north along the Kamo River before climbing into the mountains. Murakami keeps the facility itself fictional, but the route is specific enough that readers have tried to retrace it — the leading theory points toward the Hira mountains on the Shiga side, near Lake Biwa, where a real prewar sanatorium called Hiraen once operated. Treat this as an educated guess rather than a confirmed location; what's solid is the Kyoto starting point and the drive north along the Kamo River into genuinely remote, mountainous terrain.

- **Getting there**: Start from Sanjo, Kyoto, and follow the Kamo River north by car or local bus — this becomes rural quickly once you're past Ohara.
- **Good to know**: This is a scenic drive more than a pinpoint destination — there's no marked "Ami Hostel" site to visit. Go for the landscape described in the book (mountain road, river, isolation) rather than expecting a specific building.

## Practical travel notes

- **Best time to visit**: Waseda's campus is pleasant year-round; the Kyoto mountain route is best avoided in heavy winter snow.
- **Base yourself in**: central Tokyo for Waseda; Kyoto city for the mountain drive.
- **The book itself**: the standard English translation is Jay Rubin's, published by Vintage International.
